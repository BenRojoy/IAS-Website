VIDEO GAMES AS SOLUTION-YIELDERS Ever heard of a collaborative video-game called Foldit? It was a remarkable moment in history when researchers were unable to determine the structure of the protein that caused AIDs in rhesus monkeys. The research which lasted for 10 years came to an end when scientists of the University of Washington developed Foldit, an elaborate video game where the gamers were able to crack the protein structure in just a matter of 10 days. A 2010 paper in the science journal Nature credited Foldit's 57,000 players with providing useful results that outperformed algorithmically computed solutions.

WHEN VIDEO-GAMES ESTABLISHED UNITY There was a time when 'Conspiracy For Good', a transcommunication basic video game founded by Tim Kring with the support of Nokia brought together millions of people from around the World. The mobile game had 9 million users globally who came together, met each other at streets of London after 4 weeks of gaming and put their entire effort in fulfilling the goal of the game, which was to stock an African library with books for the students. The participants took things to the next level by taking tasks such as cleaning several community parks seriously, constructing 5 additional libraries, funding 50 scholarships for schoolgirls and generating over 10,000 books for the Zambian libraries through WeGiveBooks.org. This was a key event that occurred and received global attention as it enhanced the opportunities harvested through video games that could result in community-welfare.

4. GAMING FOR EDUCATION Cooperative gameplay has proved to develop problem-solving skills, team spirit, optimism, confidence and acceptance towards failures. Curriculum-based educational systems have relied upon games considering the diminished concentration among pupils. Engaging students by incorporating games within lessons keeps them focused. Nevertheless, the objective of the educational systems is the create a generation adaptable and skillful. Todays' children are tomorrows' promise.
